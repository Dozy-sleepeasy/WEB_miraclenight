Sleeping well
despite the dryness

by Ciara

That's because it's dry. It would be difficult to survive a cold winter without using the heating system. It is not uncommon for a room to become dry quickly when the heating is turned on. It is because, when the temperature in a room increases as a result of heating, the saturation vapor pressure (the amount of water that air can hold) increases, and as a result, relatively little water vapor exists within the room. Our sleep is adversely affected by this dryness in the long run.

When we sleep in a dry room for a long time, the nasal mucosa, which is a part of the respiratory system, dries out. Aside from respiratory problems, this can also lead to sleep disorders like sleep apnea. In the event that these symptoms worsen, apnea and wakefulness repeat throughout the night, resulting in sleep disruption, thereby increasing the likelihood of insomnia. 1)

So, how do we deal with dryness?

A humidity of 60% is recommended for indoor spaces to prevent dryness. In order to increase humidity, you can place a humidifier next to your bed or place wet towels around the room. Thus, it keeps the nasal mucosa moist and promotes deep sleep.

In the colder months of the year, when the nights are long, melatonin, a hormone that induces sleep, is secreted in greater amounts. Having a good night's sleep becomes increasingly important as we sleep more. If you are unable to sleep because you feel uncomfortable in bed, why not check the humidity level in the room?
